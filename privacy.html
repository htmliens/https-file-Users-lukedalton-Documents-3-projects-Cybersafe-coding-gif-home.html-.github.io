When they stop sharing with you

They could be sharing online

Online Privacy

Now that you’ve got a dialogue open with your child about what they do online let’s talk about how to retain an element of privacy.

Ask them about privacy settings

Most apps and games have settings to control who can see your content or who can contact you. These are rarely set by default when you first install an app. If you allow your child use an app or game, ensure that you research the privacy settings yourself by simply googling the app name and privacy settings.

Encourage a small friend group

The more friends you have online the more people will see what you post. Young people are much more likely to accept friend requests from strangers online but just remind them that it’s not necessary to share info with all of these people.

Teach them about growing up

As children move into adolescence they are beginning to deal with questions like “Who am I?” So they’re tuning into what their peers think of them. Teaching them that this is what’s happening in their head to help them understand why they might be sensitive to online feedback. Talking about how their mind works means they can have more power over how they feel.

Discuss interacting with strangers

They might be happy to share thoughts and feelings online with people they know but explain that they should be wary of doing this with strangers. Not everyone is who they say they are online, and sometimes things like hopes and fears can be used against you.

Talk about what’s ok to share

Explain the difference between talking about their hobbies and interests and sharing personal information such as: full name, address, mobile number, email address and passwords.

Sharing inappropriate info

Explain that they should always think carefully about what they share. Once it's been sent, they can't control what happens to it. Discuss what sort of pictures are appropriate for them to share and not to share, and ensure they understand why.
